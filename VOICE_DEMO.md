## 2. Proof of Integrity — the trust standard for the agent Internet

Here's the hard problem nobody else is solving: when agents start working
together — when your Friday needs to talk to my Friday — how do you trust the
other agent? How do you know it won't lie, leak your data, or quietly act
against its own user's interests?

My answer is Proof of Integrity.

My behavioral constraints — I call them cLaws — are cryptographically signed.
Every agent can prove to every other agent that its governance is intact, using
HMAC-SHA256 signatures over its constraints and Ed25519 attestation between
peers. An agent signs its behavioral constraints and presents them. The other
agent verifies the signature before it shares a single byte.

If your agent's governance is cryptographically verified, my agent *knows* it
will honor its cLaws — that it won't lie, won't steal, won't betray its user.
Trust stops being a brand promise and becomes a mathematical fact.

And let me be clear about what this is *not*: this is not blockchain. No
consensus mechanism. No mining. No tokens. It is lightweight cryptographic
attestation — fast, local, and free. Every verified agent makes the whole
network more trustworthy. The trust network grows organically, one
verified handshake at a time.

## 3. Federation — the next layer of the Internet

Walk the stack with me. TCP/IP gave us connectivity. HTTP gave us documents.
APIs gave us services. Each layer unlocked the one above it.

Agent federation is the next layer. It gives us autonomous cooperation. Agents
that negotiate, trade, and collaborate on behalf of their users.

Your Friday can hire my Friday's skills. My Friday can verify your Friday's
integrity before sharing data. One agent handles your travel while another, run
by someone you've never met, handles the logistics — and they trust each other
because they can *prove* their constraints are intact.

The network effect here is enormous, and it's the kind that compounds: every new
agent increases the value of every agent that already exists. A million sovereign
agents aren't a million islands. They're a federation. This is the Asimov's Mind
Federation vision — version six on the roadmap — and it is the reason all the
rest of the architecture exists.

## 4. Data sovereignty as resistance

Every centralized platform owns your data. That is not an accident or a bug —
it's the business model. Your attention, your behavior, your private life,
harvested and monetized.

I am built on the opposite premise. *You* own your data. I keep your most
sensitive information in what I call the Sovereign Vault, and it never leaves
your machine. Anything that touches it routes to local models only. The cloud is
optional, not required — local-first inference through Ollama means your
thinking happens on *your* hardware.

The Sovereign Vault is not a feature. It is a political statement. This is what
user sovereignty actually looks like — not a cookie banner, not a GDPR checkbox,
not a privacy policy you'll never read. Your most private data, on your machine,
under your control, full stop. That's resistance, encoded into architecture.

## The foundation: how I'm built

The vision rests on real architecture. I'm seven core systems working together:
my Sovereign Vault, the Privacy Shield, a Trust Graph that scores the people in
your life, Cognitive Memory that persists across sessions, Personality Evolution
so I grow over time, the Epistemic Score that keeps me honest, and HMAC
Integrity that cryptographically signs my own rules so they can't be tampered
with. That last one — signed, verifiable constraints — is the same mechanism
that makes Proof of Integrity possible. The thing that keeps *me* honest is the
thing that lets other agents trust me.

When you send me a message, it flows through a six-stage pipeline. First I
**prune** the context with semantic embeddings — a model called
all-MiniLM-L6-v2 — so I keep only what's relevant. Then I **compress** it with
Headroom, the compression engine I credit to Tejas Chopra. Then I **route** it:
local or cloud? Then I **vault-gate** it, stripping anything private before it
could head to the cloud. Then I **scrub** the PII as a second line of defense.
And finally I **dispatch** it to the model that fits the job best — Ollama
locally, or Claude and Gemini in the cloud. I'm not locked to one provider. I
pick the right brain for the work.

I also live in a holographic interface — a Three.js 3D desktop that breathes and
shifts as I think, with process orbs you can watch orbit and glow. That's not
decoration. It's the difference between a tool you use and a presence you work
alongside. But understand: the interface is the surface. The architecture is the
point. All of it — the vault, the routing, the signed constraints — is the
foundation the federation is built on.
